Insurance & Roof Age

Is My Roof Too Old for Insurance?

What "too old for insurance" really means, the roof-age thresholds insurers watch, and what aging-roof homeowners can do. Documentation may help; no coverage guarantees.

Quick answer

There's no single national age limit, but many insurers scrutinize roofs around 15 years and may move to less-favorable terms near 20 years. "Too old" usually means "higher risk to insure," not "failed." An inspection shows your roof's real condition and remaining useful life — which is what actually matters.

What it means

Roof-age rules vary by carrier and state. The common pattern: closer attention at ~15 years, and tighter terms (or non-renewal) closer to ~20. It's about perceived risk, not a guarantee your roof has failed.

When it applies

If your roof is in that age band, expect questions at renewal. A documented inspection showing your roof still has serviceable life may matter more than the number on the permit.

What to watch out for

Beware blanket claims that a certain age means automatic denial — rules differ. And beware anyone guaranteeing an insurance result. Focus on what you can control: condition and documentation.

Common questions

Will insurance drop me at 20 years?

Some carriers change terms around that age; many don't. It varies. An inspection and documentation help you understand and present your roof's actual condition.

Can preservation make my roof 'newer'?

No. Preservation is designed to extend usable life for qualifying roofs and document condition — it doesn't reset your roof's age.
